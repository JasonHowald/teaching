Course Information

Credit Hours: 3

Schedule: MWF 12:00-12:50, Carson 0208

Prerequisites: none

Required textbooks/materials: Openintro Statistics, Fourth Edition (An open textbook.)

Course Description: Elementary probabilistic and descriptive statistical concepts as applied to practical problems from other disciplines and an introduction to methods of statistical inference. Prerequisite: high school algebra or equivalent. Topics will include interpretation and analysis of data, calculation probabilities, calculation of mean, median, mode, standard deviation, and variance, fundamental counting principle, Central Limit Theorem, normal and binomial distributions, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing.

Student Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this course, a student will

  1. be able to distinguish between and a sample and population and then determine whether a numerical value is a statistic or parameter.
  2. be able to calculate the mean, median, and mode for a data and decide which best represents the data set.
  3. be able to calculate standard deviation and understand that the larger the standard deviation, the more varied the data values.
  4. be able to determine whether or not two events are mutually exclusive.
  5. know the Fundamental Counting Principle and whether or not an arrangement of objects is a permutation or combination and be able to count them.
  6. know what a discrete and continuous random variables are.
  7. recognize a normal distribution and be able to calculate probabilities using z-scores and tables or technology.
  8. know the Central Limit Theorem and how to apply it and understand the standard deviation for the sampling distribution of sample means is less than that for the original distribution.
  9. be able to construct confidence intervals for means and proportions and be able to interpret them.
  10. be able to perform an hypothesis test for a mean using a normal or t-distribution and understand how to interpret the decision in the context of the problem.

AI Policy:

Students are encouraged to leverage AI tools, such as Jupyter with code completion features or chatbots for learning help, to enhance their understanding of mathematical concepts and streamline computational tasks. All interactions with AI systems are consultations with an external source, just like a book or another person. Copying the work of an AI system and submitting it as ones own, even with some changes, is a form of plagiarism. Calling on AI for solutions to assigned problems is cheating, for the same reason that hiring your Mom to do your homework is cheating.

Thinking Mathematically:

Thinking Mathematically courses build proficiency with fundamental tools of mathematics, such as arithmetic, algebra, geometry, functions, graphs, and statistics. They teach students precise quantitative logical reasoning and applications of mathematical problem-solving skills in abstract and real-world problems. They engage students in oral and written communication of mathematical ideas.

Math 125 fulfills the Thinking Mathematically requirement of the Pathways Core Curriculum.

Thinking Mathematically Student Learning Outcomes:

At the conclusion of this course, students…

  1. Use the fundamental tools of mathematics, such as arithmetic, algebra, geometry, functions, graphs, and statistics. (Applying)
  2. Apply precise, logical quantitative reasoning to mathematical hypotheses and conditions. (Applying)
  3. Identify mathematical structure and pattern. (Applying)
  4. Engage in mathematical abstractions. (Applying)
  5. Communicate effectively with oral and written skills appropriate to the discipline. (Understanding)

Methods of Instruction in relation to SLOs:

SLO #1: Students use boxplots, histograms, scatterplots, and other visualizations to analyze data sets. Students use arithmetic and algebra to construct confidence intervals and perform hypothesis testing. Students use functions and arithmetic to calculate descriptive statistics (mean, median, etc) of datasets. Students use statistics throughout the course.

SLO #2: Students solve verbal problems distinguishing between correlation and causation on homework and exams. They verify conditions such as near-normality and sample size for hypothesis tests. They answer homework questions on experiment design which require that they attend to the experimental conditions necessary to draw various conclusions.

SLO #3: Students learn to use basic data analysis techniques to discover patterns in data. The central limit theorem provides a recurring pattern which students use to solve problems involving p-values, including hypothesis testing, on homework and exams.

SLO #4: Students use the empirical rule to produce confidence intervals for individual data points and later for parameters based on inference. To understand the general formulations of these rules, students must think of datasets generically and abstractly. On most homeworks, students use formulas for statistics, tests, and inferences which are expressed abstractly to perform concrete computations. The notions of statistic and parameter are themselves abstractions of data computations.

SLO #5: Students discuss Statistics problems and their solutions in collaborative group work. They present solutions to their peers in the classroom. They also submit individual written work in the form of homework, quizzes, and exams.

SLO’s #1-#5 will be assessed on various homework problems.

Disruptive Student Policy

In order to maintain a fair, just, and safe College community environment, students are subject to SUNY Potsdam College regulations and are expected to abide by the Potsdam Pledge, the SUNY Potsdam Academic Catalog, and the Student Community Rights and Responsibilities (the Student Code of Conduct). Anyone who disrupts the academic learning environment (e.g., classrooms, labs, office hours, online platforms, emails, or other virtual communications) will be asked to leave for the day. Disruptions may include, but are not limited to, interrupting/speaking over other people, excessive use of foul or abusive or derogatory language, and repeated getting up/leaving/returning behavior. Students who exhibit aggressive/violent behavior in or out of classrooms will be asked to immediately leave. Disruptive students may be subject to disciplinary action, potentially including administrative removal from the course.
