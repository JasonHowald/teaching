4-5. Sampling and Sample Bias

…and all my friends agree with me.

Methods of sampling, types of sample bias, and the language of good and bad sampling.

1 Preparation

Read section 1.3 – Sampling principles and strategies. Review vocabulary on next two slides.

2 Key vocabulary – sampling

  • Population: All conceivable cases.
  • Sample: All cases measured and recorded.
  • Representative Sample: Does a good job reflecting features of the population.
  • Biased Sample: Skewed, slanted, not representative. Intentionally or accidentally.
  • Bias: Anything causing a sample to be biased, from cruelty to oversight to unforeseeable effect.

3 Key vocabulary – bias types

  • Participation bias or nonresponse bias: You didn’t measure those who didn’t respond, and it might matter!
  • Pre-screening bias: You must be this tall for the survey.
  • Survivorship bias: Only “survivors” are included.
  • Undercoverage bias: Significant subpopulations were missed.
  • Geographic bias: Selecting from a town, street corner, etc.

4 A pollster’s dilemma

Which of these should a political polling organization accomplish in order to responsibly estimate the outcome of an upcoming election?

  1. Interview lots and lots of people.
  2. Include a diversity of people (in age, race, hometown, education, etc) roughly in accord with the population.
  3. Predict who probably will not vote in the election.
  4. Prompt the “undecided” to predict their decision.
  5. Draw no inference when people refuse to participate.

(Good political polling is very, very hard!)

10 Key vocabulary – Sampling strategies

  • Simple (SRS): Select from one flat list.
  • Stratified: Identify strata of interest, sample some of each stratum.
  • Cluster: Divide into convenient clusters. Randomly select some clusters. Take all of each selected cluster.
  • Multistage: Like cluster, but take some of each selected cluster.

11 Identifying the sampling strategy

You want to survey college students about relationship viewpoints. Ana: We’ll each pick a residence hall and knock ten random doors within it. Biljana: Let’s ask the registrar for a list of 100 random current students. Then we can email them! Marija: Let’s coordinate with five random professors, and survey everybody in their classes. Dragan: It may depend on relationship status. We need to get a sample with the right number of people who are single, dating, married, etc. Bonus: To generate their names I picked a nation at random, then chose four random names common within that nation. What sampling strategy did I use?
